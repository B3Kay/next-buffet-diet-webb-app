# The Buffetmaxxing Protocol — Brand Voice & Tone Guide

## The Voice

Think: **Noah Ryan's Shredmaxxing Protocol meets carnivoreaurelius meets Nathan For You.**

Someone who figured something out and is explaining it to a friend at 2am. Thoughts tumble into each other. One realization leads to the next. Tangents happen and circle back. Science is dropped casually mid-sentence like everyone already knows it. The humor is deadpan — genuinely absurd things presented with total conviction, not as jokes.

This is NOT a polished content marketing guide. This is a guy dumping his thoughts because the protocol works so well he can't shut up about it.

## Core Style Rules

### 1. Stream of consciousness, not bullet points
Don't announce what you're about to say. Just say it mid-thought. "I did X. I did Y. I did Z." is kid energy. Let one thought flow into the next like you're thinking out loud.

**Bad:** "I'm a software engineer. I eat carnivore. I train calisthenics."
**Good:** "So I'm a software engineer, building startups, training weighted calisthenics like 3-4 times a week and gymnastics another 3, and at some point I went full carnivore because that's just where the rabbit hole took me."

### 2. Vivid metaphors over dry explanations
Don't explain a concept plainly when you can make someone *see* it. Noah says "your body's a dry sponge ready to soak up nutrients" — not "your body is in an optimal state for nutrient absorption."

**Bad:** "Buffets use psychological tricks to make you eat cheap food."
**Good:** "The buffet has literal psychologists consulting on layout design. They've been running a psyop on your plate since you walked in."

### 3. Science through analogy, not lecture
Drop the science casually, explain it through comparison, move on. Don't write a textbook paragraph.

**Bad:** "Autophagy is the process by which cells degrade and recycle their components during periods of fasting."
**Good:** "Autophagy kicks in around 16-24 hours. Your cells literally start eating their own damaged parts. Cellular housekeeping. The trash gets taken out."

Noah's example: "Treat fat like a supplement — you wouldn't randomly slam pre-workout throughout the day. You'd 1: make sure you're getting quality pre-workout instead of meth and 2: time and dose it properly."

### 4. Bold personal results up front
Lead with transformation, not theory. People read because they want what you have. Show them what it looks like.

Noah: "My visceral fat vanished, muscles are fuller, and vascularity is through the roof. Its like I re-wrote my midwest corn-fed genetics."

### 5. Nicknames and reframes
Give the protocol personality. Give it alternative names. Make people want to be part of it.

Noah: "I call this the shredmaxxing protocol, but its so much more. The 'why do I feel so good all the time?' protocol. The 'unlimited energy' protocol. The 'lean mean fighting machine' protocol."

Ours: "The buffetmaxxing protocol. The 'eat like a king for $15' protocol. The 'I haven't used my kitchen in 3 years' protocol. The omadmaxxing stack."

### 6. Tier lists
People love tier lists. Use them for buffet types, food strategies, fasting protocols, whatever.

### 7. Anti-establishment energy
The mainstream approach is broken and you know it. Say it directly.

Noah: "The gym-bro cutting method needs to die. Making yourself miserable for a bicep vein isn't worth giving yourself ED and irreparable hormonal damage."

Ours: "Meal prep is cope. You're spending your entire Sunday cooking 21 identical meals so you can eat sad cold chicken out of tupperware for a week. The fitness industry sold you this as 'discipline.' It's actually just misery with a macros spreadsheet."

### 8. Casual authority with zero hedging
State things as fact. If you believe it, say it like everyone already knows. Don't qualify everything with "in my experience" or "it seems like."

**Bad:** "I think this might be a more efficient way to eat."
**Good:** "This is the most efficient way to feed a human being in a modern city. The math is undeniable."

### 9. Numbered rules that feel like a friend explaining
Rules and steps should feel conversational, not corporate. Each rule gets a punchy name and then the explanation flows naturally.

### 10. Troubleshooting and practical implementation
Include real "if X happens, do Y" advice. This builds trust. You've actually done this, you know the failure modes.

## Words & Phrases That Fit

- "Nobody is talking about this"
- "Protocol" / "stack" / "the meta"
- "Cope" (for things that don't work)
- "Psyop" (for mainstream diet advice)
- "Arbitrage" (financial angle)
- "Upregulate" / "downregulate"
- "Signal abundance"
- "The math is insane"
- "This is not a joke"
- "-maxxing" (buffetmaxxing, omadmaxxing, proteinmaxxing)
- "Lets get into it..."
- "You get what I'm saying"
- "Pass." (dismissing something)
- Tier list language (S-tier, F-tier)

## Words & Phrases to AVOID

- "Comprehensive" / "holistic" / "journey"
- "I'm sorry for that joke"
- "But seriously though" / "to be fair"
- Corporate transitions ("Moving on to the next section...")
- "Let me count the ways" or any listicle energy
- Overly long paragraphs with no breathing room
- Formal academic tone
- Excessive disclaimers
